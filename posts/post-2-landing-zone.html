Azure Landing Zones: A Practical Architect's View

Landing zones are about enablement, not control for its own sake. The goal is to give teams a governed place to build without forcing every workload team to rediscover identity, networking, monitoring, and policy from scratch.

The core idea

An enterprise landing zone creates a foundation for cloud adoption. It usually includes management group structure, policy assignment, network topology, identity integration, logging, security tooling, and subscription vending patterns.

Useful boundaries

Area Platform owns Workload owns
Identity Tenant integration, privileged access, RBAC guardrails. Application roles, managed identity use, least-privilege access.
Network Hub, routing, shared ingress and egress controls, DNS baseline. Spoke design, subnet needs, private endpoints, workload flows.
Governance Policies, tags, budgets, logging requirements, allowed regions. Architecture decisions within the approved guardrails.

Key principles

  • Separate platform subscriptions from workload subscriptions.
  • Keep policy opinionated enough to prevent common mistakes, but not so tight that teams build around it.
  • Make networking and identity patterns easy to consume.
  • Design for scale, but start with the controls that matter for the first workloads.
Architect's test: a landing zone is working when teams can deploy faster because the hard shared decisions are already handled.
